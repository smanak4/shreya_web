Price Tag

WE DON'T NEED YOUR MONEY

Sorry Jessie, in order to survive in the real world you need money. I don’t really care if you want the world to dance. It is extremely naive to believe that dancing is somehow more beneficial than money. There is a clear idealization presented in the song - people can look beyond the presentation of wealth. While Jessie focuses on critiquing the music industry’s obsession with accumulating more wealth, her statements reflect the consequences of the 2008 recession.

WHAT DOES JESSIE J NEED?

The opening line “seems like everyone’s got a price” resonates with an audience that is largely middle class. According to research done by the Stanford Center on Poverty and Inequality, the middle class lost the most of their wealth proportionally compared to the upper and lower classes. While the upper class was able to quickly recover their losses because most of their wealth was in the stock market, the middle class most definitely did not recover by 2010. The middle class was forced to enjoy life without acknowledging their price tags. Jessie reassures the people who are not wealthy that she as an artist is not looking for consumers, but is instead hoping to make the world dance.

Jessie clearly wants to empower people without the use of money and wealth, which did make sense to say in terms of the economic environment at that point in time. Realistically, the music industry wants to make a profit and the most relatable songs will help make the profit. While Jessie claims that she doesn't care about money people pay for her songs, this song likely made her a lot of money.
